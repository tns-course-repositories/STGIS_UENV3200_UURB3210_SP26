ğŸ§­ Course Description

Through weekly lectures, guided lab exercises, independent research, and a culminating final project, students gain a working knowledge of urban spatial analysis and urban analytics using Geographic Information Systems (GIS). The course examines how spatial data and analytical methods are used to analyze cities, neighborhoods, infrastructure, and environmental systems, with applications across planning, policy, design, public health, housing, climate resilience, and social equity. As GIS and spatial analytics are foundational tools across urban-focused disciplines, students develop practical experience with contemporary GIS software while learning to critically interpret and communicate spatial patterns and relationships.

Lecture sessions introduce the history, theory, and analytical capabilities of GIS as they relate to urban systems and spatial decision-making. Weekly lab demonstrations support technical skill development in spatial data acquisition, management, analysis, and visualization. Students complete applied assignments that build on lecture and reading materials. In the latter portion of the course, students design and execute an independent urban analytics project aligned with course themes, demonstrating the integration of conceptual frameworks with technical GIS workflows through spatial analyses and cartographic outputs that address real-world urban questions.

Classes meet on consecutive Tuesdays from 4:00â€“6:40pm. In-person sessions are used to introduce weekly materials, review prior work, and prepare students for upcoming assignments. Class meetings typically begin with a review of the previous weekâ€™s themes and assignments, followed by an introduction to new workflows and discussion of relevant issues.

In addition to weekly in-person meetings, the course includes asynchronous ğŸ“˜ Lecture and ğŸ§ª Demonstration Lab components alongside a ğŸ“ Weekly Assignment. Demonstration Labs reinforce course concepts through extended, step-by-step GIS workflows aligned with weekly topics. These materials remain available throughout the semester and may be used both to support assignments and to explore alternative approaches to spatial problems and analytical methods introduced in the course.

ğŸ§© Course Themes

  • Foundations of Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and spatial thinking
  • Urban data sources and spatial data structures (vector, raster, tabular, and geocoded data)
  • Cartographic design, data visualization, and spatial storytelling
  • Spatial data management, cleaning, and integration across multiple sources
  • Urban systems and processes, including land use, housing, infrastructure, and environmental dynamics
  • Urban analytics and spatial decision-making, with emphasis on measuring, comparing, and interpreting spatial patterns across cities and neighborhoods
  • Spatial statistics and exploratory spatial data analysis (ESDA), including spatial autocorrelation, clustering, and hotspot analysis
  • Mapping vulnerability and inequity, examining how spatial data can be used to analyze environmental risk, social vulnerability, access to resources, and uneven urban outcomes
  • Applied GIS workflows for urban research, policy analysis, and design practice
  • Ethical considerations in spatial data, including data bias, representation, scale, and uncertainty

By the end of the semester, successful students will:

  • Apply spatial thinking and urban analytics to analyze and interpret spatial data related to urban systems, environmental risk, and social vulnerability.
  • Demonstrate working proficiency in GIS workflows, including spatial data acquisition, management, analysis, and visualization.

  • Analyze and map vulnerability and inequity using spatial data to examine uneven urban outcomes.
  • Translate GIS data and outputs across desktop and web-based mapping environments.

  • Apply course concepts and skills in anticipation of future academic and professional work in urban planning, policy, environmental studies, and related fields.
